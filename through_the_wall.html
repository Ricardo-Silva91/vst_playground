Physical Model
Sound bleeding through architecture.

Through the Wall simulates the acoustic behavior of a sound source separated from the listener by physical structure: a wall, a floor, a door. Four parameters model different aspects of transmission loss, room acoustics, structural resonance, and perceived distance. The result is audio that genuinely feels like it belongs in another space.

macOS Windows
Signal Chain
Input › LPF x2 › Comb Filter › Schroeder Reverb › Blend › Output

Two cascaded low-pass filters model the material absorption of the wall, attenuating high frequencies. A comb filter introduces the resonant rattling of thin surfaces (glass, drywall, doors). A Schroeder reverb models the bleed of room reflections from the other side. Distance applies a final gain and high-frequency roll-off to simulate air absorption over space.

Parameters
Wall Thickness

Controls the cutoff of the dual cascaded LPF. Low values simulate thin surfaces (a door, a partition) that let significant high-frequency content through. High values model dense concrete or brick that strips the signal down to a muffled low-end thud.

LPF cutoff: high (thin) → low (thick)
Room Bleed

Sets the mix level of the Schroeder reverb, representing the ambient reflections of the room on the other side bleeding through. Low values sound like a sound-treated space. High values suggest a large, live room where the whole environment is leaking through.

Range: 0 (dry other-side) → 1 (full room)
Wall Rattle

Drives the comb filter intensity, adding resonant peaks that simulate a surface physically vibrating. At low settings it's barely perceptible. At higher settings the effect produces the characteristic buzzing rattle of a thin door or window pane resonating with bass frequencies.

Range: 0 (no resonance) → 1 (strong rattle)
Distance

Applies an additional overall level reduction and high-frequency softening. Models the inverse-square attenuation of sound over physical space. Combined with high Wall Thickness, extreme Distance settings produce a barely audible, bass-only murmur of a very far-away source.

Range: near → very far
Use Cases
Adjacent Room in Film / Game Audio

Use on a dialogue or music stem to place it believably in an adjacent space. A character hears music playing from next door, a conversation through a thin wall. Keep Wall Rattle and Room Bleed moderate to avoid caricature. The goal is plausibility, not effect.

Wall Thickness: 0.5 / Room Bleed: 0.3 / Rattle: 0.2 / Distance: 0.4
Lo-Fi Telephone and Radio

High Wall Thickness with low Room Bleed and near-zero Rattle produces the band-limited, muffled quality of a telephone call or a cheap transistor radio. Works on vocals, guitars, or any source you want to feel mediated by a low-quality transmission path.

Wall Thickness: 0.8 / Room Bleed: 0.05 / Rattle: 0.0 / Distance: 0.2
Upstairs Neighbor Bass Thud

Max out Wall Thickness and Distance, push Wall Rattle high to get the resonant boom of a kick drum heard through a concrete ceiling. The high end is completely gone, the mids are smeared, and the bass hits with that recognizable building-shaking quality of sound felt more than heard.

Wall Thickness: 0.9 / Room Bleed: 0.4 / Rattle: 0.7 / Distance: 0.6
Distant Stage Monitor Bleed

Light Wall Thickness, moderate Distance, and some Room Bleed captures the character of a monitor speaker heard from across a live room. Useful in mix beds or cinematic scenes set in music venues, adding spatial realism without a complex room simulation.

Wall Thickness: 0.3 / Room Bleed: 0.5 / Rattle: 0.1 / Distance: 0.5
Tips and Workflow
▶

Think in layers. Wall Thickness sets what frequencies survive. Room Bleed sets how much of the receiving space is audible. Rattle adds the physical character of the barrier itself. Distance pulls the whole thing back. Adjust in that order for the most controlled results.

▶

Room Bleed without Wall Thickness is just reverb. The effect is most convincing when the LPF has removed the high frequencies first. A reverberant muffled signal sounds like a room. A reverberant bright signal just sounds like a large space.

▶

Use on a send for realism. In film or game audio, blend the processed signal at low level under the dry source to simulate a character in a space where a neighboring sound is faintly audible. The dry channel stays close; the send adds the "other room" layer.

▶

Wall Rattle pairs well with bass-heavy sources. The comb filter resonances interact with fundamental frequencies. On kick drums or bass guitars, try pushing Rattle and listening for which frequencies start to "sing" out of the wall. That's the physical resonance of the barrier material.

▶

Automate Distance for movement. Gradually increasing Distance while a sound plays simulates someone or something physically moving away. Combined with increasing Wall Thickness it tracks how a sound changes as it moves deeper into a building.
