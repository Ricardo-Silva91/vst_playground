Physical Model
The sound of music bleeding through drywall, concrete, and floors.

Through the Wall puts your audio on the other side of a wall. Not a simple lo-fi filter — a physically modelled transmission effect that replicates the four acoustic phenomena that actually happen when sound passes through a structure: high-frequency absorption, wall resonance, room bleed, and spatial distance. Dial in anything from a thin shared apartment wall to three floors of concrete.

macOS Windows
Signal Chain
Input› Wall Rattle› Room Bleed› Distance› Wall Thickness› Output

The comb filter (Wall Rattle) models sympathetic resonance first, adding the physical character of the barrier material before the room bleed is applied. The Schroeder reverb network (Room Bleed) then adds the diffuse ambience of the space on the other side. Distance applies dry attenuation and reverb scaling. Finally, the dual cascaded IIR low-pass filters (Wall Thickness) strip out high frequencies exactly as material absorption does in the real world. All four parameters are smoothed per-sample to prevent zipper noise during automation.

Parameters
Wall Thickness

Dual cascaded low-pass filters that eat into your high end the way a real wall does. At low settings you lose the air and shimmer. At max you're left with a muddy 150 Hz bloom — just low-end thud, nothing else.

LPF cutoff: high (thin wall) → low (dense concrete)
Room Bleed

A Schroeder reverb network (4 comb + 2 allpass filters) adding the diffuse ambience of the room on the other side. Crank it up and the source feels like it's sitting in a space you can't see.

Range: 0 (dry other-side) → 1 (full room)
Wall Rattle

A short comb filter (2–8 ms) that models the wall itself resonating sympathetically. The hollow, buzzy coloration that cheap drywall adds when something hits the right frequency. Subtle at low settings, nasty at high ones.

Range: 0 (no resonance) → 1 (strong rattle)
Distance

Pushes the dry signal further away while scaling up the reverb contribution. At max, the original source is almost entirely gone — just room wash and low-end surviving the journey.

Range: near → very far
Presets
Thin Partition

Cheap drywall — highs still present, barely muffled. The room next door with a thin shared wall.

Apartment Next Door

The classic shared-wall sound. Mids and highs attenuated, bleed of the neighbor's room audible.

Concrete Block

Heavy wall, almost no highs, dense low-end rumble. Industrial building or a thick exterior wall.

Upstairs Neighbor

Floor/ceiling transmission — thuddy and diffuse. Footsteps and bass come through; everything else is gone.

Rattling Pipes

Old building with loose structure. Wall Rattle is pushed high — the wall buzzes in sympathy with bass frequencies.

Ghost Room

Barely there — bass and reverb wash only. The most extreme setting. More atmosphere than signal.

Use Cases
Lo-fi and bedroom pop

Add the "heard through a wall" texture to drums, bass, or a whole bus. Works especially well as a parallel layer under the clean signal — the processed version adds subliminal spatial depth without fully muffling anything.

Wall Thickness: 0.4–0.6 / Room Bleed: 0.3–0.5 / Rattle: 0.1 / Distance: 0.3
Film and game audio

Off-screen sources, adjacent rooms, muffled interiors. A character hears music playing from next door. Keep Wall Rattle and Room Bleed moderate to avoid caricature — the goal is plausibility, not a lo-fi effect.

Wall Thickness: 0.5 / Room Bleed: 0.3 / Rattle: 0.2 / Distance: 0.4
Transition effects

Automate Wall Thickness to make a sound "push through" from one space to another. Increasing thickness over 4–8 bars then suddenly cutting to dry creates a vivid sense of moving through a physical barrier.

Wall Thickness: automate 0 → 0.8 / Distance: automate with thickness
Upstairs neighbor bass thud

Max out Wall Thickness and Distance, push Wall Rattle high to get the resonant boom of a kick drum heard through a concrete ceiling. High end is completely gone, bass hits with that building-shaking quality felt more than heard.

Wall Thickness: 0.9 / Room Bleed: 0.4 / Rattle: 0.7 / Distance: 0.6
Tips and Workflow
▶

Think in layers. Wall Thickness sets what frequencies survive. Room Bleed sets how much of the receiving space is audible. Rattle adds the physical character of the barrier itself. Distance pulls the whole thing back. Adjust in that order for the most controlled results.

▶

Room Bleed without Wall Thickness is just reverb. The effect is most convincing when the LPF has removed high frequencies first. A reverberant muffled signal sounds like a room. A reverberant bright signal just sounds like a large space.

▶

Use on a send for realism. In film or game audio, blend the processed signal at low level under the dry source. The dry channel stays close; the send adds the "other room" layer without replacing the original.

▶

Wall Rattle pairs well with bass-heavy sources. The comb filter resonances interact with fundamental frequencies. On kick drums or bass guitars, push Rattle and listen for which frequencies start to "sing" out of the wall — that's the physical resonance of the barrier material.

▶

Low CPU cost. No FFT, no oversampling — the plugin uses simple IIR filters and a Schroeder network, so it runs lightly even on older hardware. Stack multiple instances on different tracks without concern.
