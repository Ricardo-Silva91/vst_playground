Vocal Harmonizer
From tight doubles to wrecked lo-fi choir stacks.

Choir Box layers pitch-shifted voices above and below your signal, spreads them across the stereo field, and optionally runs them through a saturation and hard-clip chain before mixing. Up to eight shifted voices total, independently levelled and panned. Works on vocals, synth pads, bass, keys — anything that needs harmonic depth.

macOS Windows
Signal Chain
Input› Sat → Hard Clip› Dist Mix› Phase Vocoder› Up Voices ×1–4› Down Voices ×1–4› Stereo Spread› Output Gain› Output

The distortion section (Sat into hard clip) runs on the input before any pitch shifting. Dist Mix then blends between clean and distorted signal, and that blend feeds the phase vocoder. This means harmonic content introduced by saturation gets shifted along with the fundamental — the grit moves with the notes. The phase vocoder introduces ~46ms of processing latency, reported to the host automatically.

Pitch & Voices
Up Semitones

Semitones above the input for the upper voices. Default +7 (a perfect fifth) works on almost anything. Negative values flip the upper voices below the input for dense low clusters.

Range: −24 → +24 st    Default: +7
Down Semitones

Semitones below the input for the lower voices. Default −7 mirrors the fifth downward. Set to 0 to silence the lower layer and use only the upward voices.

Range: −24 → +24 st    Default: −7
Voices

Voice layers generated per direction. At 1 you get one up and one down voice. At 4 you get four of each — eight shifted voices total. Higher counts spread detune and panning more aggressively across the stereo field.

Range: 1 → 4    Default: 1
Detune

Random pitch offset between voices in each direction, in cents. At 0 voices are in perfect unison. Higher values create the natural looseness of a real choir — each voice sits at a slightly different pitch, just as no two singers ever lock perfectly.

Range: 0 → 100 cents    Default: 20
Levels & Distortion
Dry / Up Lvl / Down Lvl

Independent levels for the dry signal and each voice direction. Pull Dry back to 0.3 with voice levels up for a hollow, pitch-shifted texture where the source almost disappears behind its harmonics.

Dry default: 1.0    Up / Down Lvl default: 0.7
Sat

Tanh saturation before the voices are generated. Low values add warmth; high values clip aggressively. Even a small amount adds air to the shifted voices without obvious distortion on the output.

Range: 0 → 1    Default: 0
Crush

Hard clip threshold on top of Sat, flattening transients and adding a harder edge. Works best when Sat is already engaged — Crush shapes the ceiling of the already-saturated signal rather than clipping a clean source.

Range: 0 → 1    Default: 0
Dist Mix

Blends clean input with the distorted signal before pitch shifting. At 0 the voices are entirely clean; at 1 fully processed. 10–20% adds warmth without sounding distorted — the saturation becomes part of the timbre of the voices rather than an audible effect.

Range: 0 (clean) → 1 (full distortion)    Default: 0
Output

Master output gain. Four voices per direction at full level will clip — use this to compensate when stacking many voices at high levels.

Range: 0 → 2    Default: 1.0
Presets
Power Fifth

One voice up a fifth, one down. No detune, no distortion. Clean fifth harmonics that reinforce anything — pads, bass, vocals. The default starting point.

Tight Stack

Minor third up and down, two voices, light detune and a touch of saturation. Dense, slightly impure unison — ideal for thickening a lead without it sounding like an obvious effect.

Choir

Four voices, seventh up, fifth down, moderate detune and barely-there saturation. Voices fan out across the full stereo field, approximating the natural spread of a small vocal group.

Lo-Fi Choir

Three voices, heavier saturation and crush, blended halfway with the distortion mix. Like a choir recorded on cheap tape — works on sampler pads, lo-fi keys, and drum room channels.

Blown Out

Two voices, a seventh up and an octave down, heavy saturation, nearly full crush. Intentionally rough. Best as a send effect or layered quietly under a clean source for texture.

Ghost Voice

One voice up an octave, one a fifth down, dry pulled to 30%, no distortion. The source almost disappears behind its harmonics — hollow and distant. Good for pads and transitions.

Use Cases
Vocal harmonies and doubles

Set Up and Down Semitones to thirds or fifths, keep Voices at 1–2 and Detune low. The result sounds like a real double-tracked vocal or a tight harmony part — without recording a second take. Use as a send to control the blend from the mixer.

Up St: +4 or +7 / Down St: −3 or −7 / Voices: 1 / Detune: 5–15
Synth pad thickening

On a sustained pad, push Voices to 3–4 with moderate Detune and the Choir preset. Each voice gets panned to a different position, turning a single oscillator into a wide, drifting harmonic spread that fills the stereo field.

Voices: 3–4 / Detune: 25–40 / Dist Mix: 0.1 for warmth
Lo-fi texture layer

Run a drum room channel, a Rhodes, or a bass through Choir Box with the Lo-Fi Choir preset. The saturated, crushed harmonics add grit and vintage character without touching the original source — blend it quietly under the dry signal at 20–30%.

Preset: Lo-Fi Choir / blend in at low send level / keep Dry low on the return
Slow harmonic movement on pads

Automate Up/Down Semitones in real time during a sustained chord. The phase vocoder responds smoothly, slowly detuning the intervals as the chord holds — from a fifth, drifting to a fourth, then a tritone. Unsettling and effective on slow-moving material.

Automate Up St and Down St slowly / Voices: 2 / Detune: 10–20
Tips and Workflow
▶

Use as a send, not an insert. Running Choir Box on a dedicated send channel lets you control the wet blend from the mixer without touching the dry signal. This keeps the dry signal phase-perfect and lets you automate choir depth independently.

▶

Voices = 1 is the cleanest. For subtle reinforcement, stay at 1–2 voices and keep Detune low. For an obvious choir effect, push to 3–4 voices with Detune above 30. The difference between 1 and 4 voices is dramatic.

▶

Stack two instances for complex chords. One instance tuned to a third, another to a fifth, both at low voice counts and moderate levels. Mixing them in the DAW gives you control over each interval independently — more flexible than trying to get both from one instance.

▶

Automate Up/Down Semitones for movement. The pitch shifter responds to parameter changes in real time. Slowly moving the interval values during a sustained chord creates a slow detuning effect — particularly effective on pads and strings where the movement has time to develop.

▶

Dist Mix at 10–20% adds texture without obvious grit. The saturation runs on the input before pitch shifting, so the harmonic content gets shifted along with the fundamental. The distortion becomes part of the timbre of the voices, not an audible effect sitting on top.

▶

Watch output gain when stacking voices. Four voices up and four down at default levels will significantly increase the output level. Bring Output down before adding more voices — it's easier to recover level than to clean up clipping on the master.
