Drum Processor
Everything a drum hit needs to become itself.

Drum Smash runs your drums through a full destruction chain: bit crushing, saturation, vinyl noise and crackle, compression, reverb, pitch and wow flutter, and stereo shaping. Five knobs cover the essentials. All 21 parameters are accessible via your DAW's generic editor. Eight presets cover the moods.

macOS Windows
Front Panel Knobs
Drive
Saturation drive   0 – 1

Pushes harmonic distortion from subtle warmth to full clip. Drag vertically. Hold Shift for fine control. Double-click to type a value.

Crush
Bit depth   1 – 16 bit

Reduces word length. 16 = clean, 1 = full destruction. Aliasing artifacts become part of the texture at lower settings.

Noise
Vinyl noise amount   0 – 1

Adds broadband hiss gated to input signal — only fires when a drum is playing, never bleeds into silence between hits.

Comp
Compressor threshold   −60 – 0 dBFS

Lowers threshold to bring in more compression. Combines with the full compressor section (ratio, attack, release, makeup) accessible in the generic editor.

Verb
Reverb room size   0 – 1

Room size of the reverb tail — placed at the end of the chain so it reverberates the already-destroyed signal, not the clean original.

Signal Chain
Pitch / Wow› Bit Crusher› Saturation› Noise / Crackle› Transient› HPF› LPF› Compressor› Reverb› Stereo Width› Output Gain

Every stage is always active — presets and manual parameter edits dial the amounts. The chain is ordered destructively first (pitch, crush, saturate, noise) then restorative (filter, compress, spatial). Character is printed before any cleanup happens. Noise and crackle are gated by input signal level and won't fire during silence between hits.

Processing Sections (21 Parameters Total)
Crusher
Bit Depth (1–16 bit) / SR Divide (1–32×)

Reduces bit depth and sample rate to introduce digital grit and aliasing. Subtle crushing adds texture without obvious lo-fi. Extreme values produce classic 12-bit drum machine crunch.

Saturation
Drive (0–1) / Output Gain (0–2×)

Harmonic distortion with different flavors: soft-clip roundness, tape-style even harmonics, or harder asymmetric clipping. Output Gain compensates for saturation-induced level increases.

Character
Noise Amount (0–1) / Crackle Rate (0–1)

Broadband noise and random crackle gated to the drum signal. Noise adds air; Crackle adds vinyl intimacy. Neither fires during silence.

Filters
HPF (20–2000 Hz) / LPF (200–22000 Hz)

Placed after the destructive processing. HPF removes mud from saturation and low-frequency noise. LPF rolls off harshness from bit crushing. Use both to carve the character into shape.

Compressor
Threshold / Ratio (1–20:1) / Attack (0.1–200 ms) / Release (10–2000 ms) / Makeup (0–24 dB)

Post-distortion compression. Fast attack kills the transient for a smashed sound. Slow attack lets the click through then ducks the body. Acts on already-destroyed audio, compounding the effect.

Reverb
Room Size (0–1) / Wet Mix (0–1) / Damping (0–1)

Room reverb placed at the end of the destructive chain. The tail carries all the noise, grit, and saturation of the processed hit — the room sounds damaged, not pristine.

Modulation
Pitch Shift (−12–+12 st) / Wow Rate (0–8 Hz) / Wow Depth (0–50 cents)

Pitch shift transposes the entire hit. Wow applies the same random-target pitch drift as Pitch Wobble. Pitch down adds weight; combined with wow it produces unstable tape with a failing capstan.

Spatial
Stereo Width (0–2) / Transient Boost (0–1)

Stereo Width widens the processed signal on the mid-side axis (1.0 = original). Transient Boost reinforces the initial click. Dry-Wet blends the full chain against the original — use the plugin as a parallel processor.

Use Cases
Parallel drum bus processing

Set Dry-Wet to 50–70% and route your full drum bus through Drum Smash. The plugin adds character and glue without replacing the original. Saturation on the bus compresses peaks psychoacoustically, making the kit feel more cohesive in the mix.

Preset: Heavy Hitter as starting point, back off Dry-Wet to taste
Resample and destroy a loop

Feed a clean loop through fully wet and print the output as a new audio file. The processed version becomes your lo-fi source, ready to layer under the original or use standalone. Bit Crusher and Tape Warped presets work especially well.

Dry-Wet: 1.0 → print / export the result
Single hit sculpting

Use on an individual kick, snare, or hi-hat in a sampler. Each section lets you shape the exact character: HPF to remove flub, saturation to add body, compressor to control transient shape, reverb to place it in a space.

Start with filters and compressor before engaging the Crusher or Saturation
Tips and Workflow
▶

The chain order is your friend. Because destruction happens before cleanup, you can push saturation and bit crush hard and then use HPF and LPF to carve out the unusable frequencies. The filters don't undo the distortion — they shape it.

▶

Noise and crackle are gated. They only appear when the drum signal is above threshold. Push them fairly high without worrying about noise bleeding into silence between hits.

▶

Slow compressor attack for punch, fast for smash. A slow attack (30–80 ms) preserves the transient click then ducks the body. A fast attack (1–5 ms) clamps the whole hit, creating a flat, heavily processed smash. Both are useful and produce completely different results.

▶

Pitch shift down for weight. Even −1 or −2 semitones makes a kick drum sound significantly bigger. Combined with saturation, the increased fundamental pushes more energy into the low midrange where most speakers reproduce bass.

▶

FL Studio automation tip. Right-click the parameter name label (not the slider) in the generic editor to get "Create automation clip." This applies to all 21 parameters.

▶

Reverb on destroyed audio is its own thing. Because reverb is post-destruction in the chain, the tail decays through all the noise and grit. This gives it an intimate, room-mic quality rather than a pristine space.
