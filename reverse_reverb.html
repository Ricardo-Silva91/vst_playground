Spatial Effect
The swell that arrives before the sound.

Reverse Reverb captures audio in short time windows, reverses them, and runs the result through a reverb tail. The effect: a ghostly bloom that swells into each note or hit, as if the room is breathing in anticipation. A staple of ambient, cinematic, and experimental production.

macOS Windows
Signal Chain
Input › Buffer Window › Reverse › Reverb › Wet Mix › Output

Audio is continuously captured into a rolling buffer of length defined by Window ms. On each cycle, the buffer is reversed and fed into a Schroeder reverb. The reversed tail is blended with the dry signal via Wet Mix. Because the reversed audio plays out before the dry signal catches up, the bloom effect precedes the attack.

Parameters
Room Size

Controls the size of the reverb space applied to the reversed audio. Smaller values produce a tight, intimate bloom. Larger values create a vast, cathedral-like swell that lingers well past the transient.

Range: small room → large hall
Wet Mix

Blends the reversed reverb tail against the dry signal. At low values the effect is subtle, almost subliminal. At full wet, the original signal disappears entirely into the swell — useful for transition effects and ambient beds.

Range: 0 (dry) → 1 (fully wet)
Window ms

The length of the capture buffer in milliseconds. Short windows (20-80ms) give a tight, rhythmically coherent bloom that won't smear fast sequences. Long windows (200ms+) capture more of the phrase and produce massive, slow-building swells.

Range: ~20ms → ~500ms
Presets
Default

Balanced starting point. Room 0.8 / Wet 0.8 / Window 500ms.

Ghost Bloom

Large, dreamy swell with a very long tail. Best on pads and sustained chords where the build-up has time to unfold. Room 0.95 / Wet 0.9 / Window 800ms.

Subtle Breath

Gentle and transparent — the effect is present but never calls attention to itself. Good starting point for vocals and acoustic sources where you want dimension without drama. Room 0.5 / Wet 0.4 / Window 300ms.

Wash Out

Fully submerged. The dry signal is entirely replaced by the reversed bloom. Useful as a transition effect or texture layer rather than a primary treatment. Room 1.0 / Wet 1.0 / Window 1500ms.

Tight Shimmer

Short window keeps the bloom rhythmically tight and punchy. Works on drums and plucked instruments where a longer swell would smear the groove. Room 0.6 / Wet 0.7 / Window 150ms.

Use Cases
Ambient Pads and Drones

Place on a sustained pad or drone. The reversed bloom reinforces each harmonic change with a ghostly anticipatory swell, adding movement without rhythm. Works beautifully in parallel with a standard reverb on the same bus.

Room Size: 0.7+ / Wet Mix: 0.4-0.6 / Window ms: 200-400
Cinematic Drum Hits

On a snare or kick, a short window creates a tension-building rise before the impact. Keep the wet mix subtle so the attack still hits hard. This is the classic film score technique for a "breathe before the slam."

Room Size: 0.5 / Wet Mix: 0.2-0.35 / Window ms: 40-100
Vocal Transitions

Automate Wet Mix up during phrase endings or silence gaps. The tail of the reversed reverb fades out the preceding phrase backwards, creating a surreal dissolve into the next section. Great on lead vocals in electronic or neo-soul productions.

Room Size: 0.6 / Wet Mix: automate 0→0.8 / Window ms: 150-300
Full Wet Texture Generation

With Wet Mix at 1.0 and a long window, the plugin becomes a texture generator. Feed in any rhythmic or melodic source and the output is a constantly evolving, reversed smear — useful as a background layer in sound design contexts.

Room Size: 0.8 / Wet Mix: 1.0 / Window ms: 300-500
Tips and Workflow
▶

Run it in parallel. The effect blends best when you keep the dry signal fully intact on a separate track and blend the fully wet plugin return to taste. This avoids pumping at the attack.

▶

Match window to tempo. At 120 BPM, one beat is 500ms. Setting Window ms to 250ms (half a beat) gives a rhythmically locked bloom that anticipates every other hit without smearing the groove.

▶

Layer with a forward reverb after. Chain Reverse Reverb into a regular room reverb on a send. The result is a bloom into the hit, then a natural decay out of it — a complete envelope of space around each sound.

▶

Automate Wet Mix for tension. Gradually raising the wet mix over 4-8 bars before a drop or chorus builds an almost subliminal sense of anticipation. Pull it back to zero on the downbeat.

▶

Very short windows on percussion are subtle and powerful. A 20-30ms window on a snare produces a brief "whoosh" that's barely perceptible consciously but adds a sense of impact and dimension that listeners feel without identifying.
